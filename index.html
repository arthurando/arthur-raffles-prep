5 Technical Fluency Cards

Show conceptual fluency. Do NOT claim implementation in financial services — demonstrate you understand the domain deeply enough to execute.

Entity & Relationship Modelling for Family Offices

Family office CRM is fundamentally a graph problem, not a flat contact database.

Entity hierarchy: Family Group → Family Members → Entities (trusts, SPVs, holding companies) → Accounts → Assets/Positions. Each node carries its own compliance profile, jurisdiction, and access controls.

Polymorphic relationships: A single individual can simultaneously be a beneficiary of Trust A, director of HoldCo B, and LP in Fund C. The data model must handle this natively — not through workarounds.

Your proof point: At STT Mall, you built hierarchical customer segmentation with behavioural scoring across multiple dimensions. The pattern is structurally identical — entities with complex, overlapping relationships, scored and classified dynamically.

Industry reference: Salesforce Financial Services Cloud models this with “household” objects and related account hierarchies. DealCloud and 4Degrees use graph-based relationship intelligence. If Raffles uses Salesforce FSC, you should reference this specific data model.

Compliance, Audit Trails & Data Governance

Event-sourced audit trail: Every state change is an immutable event, not an overwritten row. This is non-negotiable in regulated environments (SFC, MAS). You can always reconstruct the state of any record at any point in time.

Row-level security (RLS): Relationship managers see only their families. Compliance sees everything. Investment team sees deal pipeline. Leadership sees aggregated views. This maps directly to Supabase RLS, which you have implemented at STT Mall.

Regulatory landscape:

  • Hong Kong: SFC licensing, PDPO (data privacy), AMLO (AML/KYC)
  • Singapore: MAS licensing, PDPA (data privacy), CDSA (corruption/drug trafficking proceeds)
  • Cross-border: Data residency requirements may prevent single-region deployment

Key vocabulary to use: Suitability assessment, investment mandate tracking, beneficial ownership, Know Your Customer (KYC), Anti-Money Laundering (AML), Suspicious Transaction Report (STR).

Deal Flow as a State Machine

Family offices track investment opportunities through a deterministic pipeline — not freeform status strings.

Typical stages: Sourcing → Initial Review → Due Diligence → IC/Family Review → Commitment → Closing → Post-Investment Monitoring

Each transition should have: mandatory fields, approval gates, timestamp/audit, notification triggers.

Your proof point: At Emerald Media/KKR, you lived this pipeline daily. You built the IC presentations that represented the decision gates. You understand that deal flow is not a to-do list — it is a governed process with institutional accountability at each stage.

Integration Layer — Where CRM Projects Actually Fail

Most family office CRM implementations fail not on the CRM itself but on integrations:

  • Custodian feeds: Pershing, Interactive Brokers, private banks — each with proprietary APIs, FIX protocol, or flat-file feeds with 6-month onboarding cycles
  • Market data: Bloomberg terminal integration, Reuters, pricing feeds
  • Document management: Secure storage with version control, client portal distribution
  • Accounting/reporting: CRMs handle relationships; financial reporting requires reconciled accounting data (FundCount, Addepar, Masttro)

Your proof point: At STT Mall, you integrated Klaviyo, Shopify, Supabase, SendGrid, and multiple payment/logistics APIs into a unified operational stack. Integration architecture is a core competency.

The methodology is about how I structure work and make decisions, not about running client data through external systems.”

Build vs. Buy — The Right Framework

This is likely the most important positioning question. Do NOT propose building a CRM from scratch.

The right answer: “The core CRM should be a proven platform (Salesforce Financial Services Cloud, Wealth Dynamix, DealCloud) configured for your specific entity model and workflows. Where I add value is in three areas:”

  1. Integration layer — connecting the CRM to custodians, market data, reporting systems, and your RFO app
  2. Internal tooling — automations, dashboards, workflow optimisations that are too specific for off-the-shelf
  3. AI-powered features — relationship intelligence, automated insights, client engagement recommendations (prototyped and validated before production)

Why this positioning wins: It shows you understand that a regulated environment demands proven, auditable platforms for core functions — while demonstrating you can add value through customisation, integration, and innovation on top of that foundation.

6 AI Proposals for Discussion

These are conversation starters, not sales pitches. Only raise if the discussion naturally turns to technology or AI. Frame as “ideas worth exploring” — not “here’s what I would build.”

Industry context: 3x more family offices are leveraging AI in 2025 vs 2024. Top investment priorities for 2026: AI/automation and data/reporting split evenly. Expect agentic interfaces embedded in dashboards and the rise of “hybrid operator” roles blending domain expertise with AI oversight. This is the role Arthur is interviewing for.
Proposal 1: Relationship Intelligence Layer

The problem: As Raffles scales (new RMs, new markets, new clients), institutional knowledge about relationships gets lost. Who introduced whom? Which families have co-invested? What are the connection patterns between clients?

The AI opportunity: A relationship intelligence layer that automatically captures and maps connections from emails, meeting notes, and deal activity. Not replacing the RM’s judgment — augmenting their memory. When a new RM joins (like Tung and Liang just did), they get instant context on every family’s history and network.

Privacy-first approach: All processing on-premise or private cloud. No client data leaves the organisation’s infrastructure. Rule-based matching first, ML layer only for pattern recognition on anonymised metadata.

Industry precedent: DealCloud and 4Degrees are built entirely around this concept. Salesforce Einstein offers it within FSC. The question is whether Raffles wants to adopt a platform solution or build a proprietary layer that becomes a competitive moat.

Your proof point: Built behavioural scoring and automated segmentation for 55K+ subscribers at STT Mall. The pattern — ingesting interaction data, classifying relationships, triggering personalised actions — is the same.

Proposal 2: Intelligent Client Onboarding & KYC Workflow

The problem: Onboarding a UHNW family is a compliance-heavy, multi-jurisdiction process. Document collection, identity verification, beneficial ownership mapping, source-of-wealth validation — each jurisdiction (HK, SG, UAE, Rwanda) has different requirements.

The AI opportunity: An intelligent onboarding workflow that: (1) identifies which documents are needed based on family structure and jurisdictions, (2) extracts and validates key data from submitted documents using document AI, (3) flags anomalies or missing information before compliance review, (4) maintains an audit trail of every step.

Privacy-first approach: Document processing via on-premise OCR and extraction models. No client documents uploaded to external APIs. Human-in-the-loop for all compliance decisions — AI assists, never decides.

Why this matters now: Raffles is expanding to UAE and Rwanda. Each new jurisdiction multiplies the onboarding complexity. Automating the 80% that is mechanical (document collection, data extraction, checklist completion) frees compliance staff for the 20% that requires judgment.

Proposal 3: AI-Powered Client Insights for the RFO App

The problem: Raffles’ RFO app is on its 3rd upgrade with a proprietary AI function. The question is: what’s the next evolution that deepens client engagement?

The AI opportunity: Personalised insights surfaced through the app: (1) portfolio-relevant news and market movements tailored to each family’s holdings and interests, (2) event and opportunity alerts based on the family’s investment mandate and deal flow preferences, (3) natural language queries — “show me my Hong Kong real estate exposure across all entities” — instead of navigating dashboards.

Privacy-first approach: The AI model runs within Raffles’ infrastructure. Client queries and portfolio data never leave the secure environment. Insights are generated by matching public market data against private portfolio positions — only the matching logic touches sensitive data.

Competitive advantage: Most family office apps are portfolio reporting dashboards. An AI-powered insight engine that proactively surfaces opportunities and risks — tailored to each family — becomes a reason clients stay with Raffles over competitors.

Proposal 4: Cross-Entity Reporting Automation

The problem: A single UHNW family might have 10+ entities (personal accounts, trusts, SPVs, holdcos, LPs) across 4 jurisdictions. Producing a consolidated family view requires pulling data from multiple custodians, reconciling currencies, and presenting it coherently.

The AI opportunity: Automated data ingestion from custodian feeds (structured and unstructured) with AI-powered reconciliation. The system learns each custodian’s reporting format, maps positions to a unified schema, and flags discrepancies for human review. Result: consolidated family reports generated in hours instead of days.

Why this matters: This is the #1 operational pain point for multi-family offices. Every RM hour spent on manual reconciliation is an hour not spent on client relationships. Automating reporting directly improves the client experience AND the RM’s productivity.

Your proof point: At STT Mall, you integrated Klaviyo, Shopify, Supabase, SendGrid, and multiple payment/logistics APIs into a unified data pipeline. Multi-source data integration and reconciliation is a core competency.

How to present these: Do NOT open with “I have 4 AI proposals.” Instead, listen for their pain points, then selectively mention the most relevant idea as “something I’ve been thinking about” or “one area where I see an opportunity.” If they bite, go deeper. If they don’t, move on. The goal is to demonstrate you think about their business strategically — not to pitch.

10 Landmines — What NOT to Say

DO NOT say “I built a CRM”
You built a marketing automation and customer intelligence platform for e-commerce. That’s impressive, but it is not a wealth management CRM. Overclaiming here will destroy credibility with anyone who has used Salesforce FSC or DealCloud. Say: “I built a customer intelligence platform with entity modelling and segmentation — the architectural patterns translate directly.”
DO NOT propose replacing their existing stack
They already have an app on its 3rd upgrade. They have existing systems. You are there to augment, integrate, and improve — not to tear everything down and rebuild. Frame your value as additive.
DO NOT lead with AI before understanding their problems
AI is a solution. They have not told you the problem yet. Ask first, then selectively mention AI capabilities where relevant. Leading with “I have an AI methodology” in a family office context risks sounding like a solution in search of a problem.
DO NOT mention routing data through external LLM APIs
UHNW families will not tolerate their data touching third-party AI services. If asked about AI, emphasise on-premise/private-cloud models and rule-based automation first. Your personal AI methodology is for YOUR development process, not for processing client data.
DO NOT undersell regulatory complexity
Never say “we can just use Supabase” or “compliance is just another feature.” Show respect for the regulatory environment. Use phrases like: “This requires close collaboration with your compliance team” and “The governance framework should drive the technical architecture, not the other way around.”
DO NOT describe Claudia/Max by name
In a family office context, saying “I have an AI Chief of Staff named Claudia and an execution partner named Max” sounds whimsical. Frame it as: “I’ve developed a structured AI-augmented development methodology that includes multi-perspective decision analysis and automated quality gates.”

Interview Strategy

Raffles Family Office — CRM Project & Beyond

Synthesised from Strategic Council deliberation (4/5 consensus), public intelligence on 20+ Raffles personnel, industry analysis, and Arthur’s 18-year career mapping. This is your playbook.

1 Core Thesis — Why You, Why Now

The One Sentence

“I am the operator who codes — the only candidate who has run a HKD 120M business, sat in KKR deal rooms, led operational transformations at Proudfoot, AND built the AI-driven technology that powered all of it.”

Why This Thesis Wins

vs Traditional Developer
You understand the business
  • A developer builds what they’re told. You build what’s actually needed.
  • You’ve sat in the investor’s chair, the consultant’s chair, the operator’s chair
  • You can translate between what a RM needs and what a system should do
  • No requirements document needed — you can diagnose the problem yourself
vs Management Consultant
You ship working systems
  • Consultants deliver slide decks. You deliver working software.
  • Proudfoot trained you in the methodology. STT Mall proved you execute.
  • Your “consultant phase” makes you credible. Your “builder phase” makes you useful.
  • No handoff risk — you diagnose AND implement
vs Salesforce Partner
You think beyond configuration
  • A Salesforce partner configures what exists. You see what’s missing.
  • Integration layer, custom AI features, internal tooling — the 30% that makes the difference
  • You can evaluate vendors, not just implement them
  • You understand build vs buy trade-offs from running your own business
vs In-House Hire
Zero ramp-up time on business context
  • A junior hire takes 6–12 months to understand wealth management
  • You bring Bloomberg treasury, ANZ corporate banking, KKR PE — day one
  • You speak the language: deal flow, IC presentations, AUM, mandates, suitability
  • Trilingual (EN/Cantonese/Mandarin) covering their entire APAC footprint

4 Conversation Flow — Steering the Interview

You can’t control the questions, but you can control the arc. Here’s the ideal flow.

First 5 minutes

Establish Credibility Fast

Lead with the institutional signal: “I’ve spent 18 years at the intersection of finance and technology — from Bloomberg and ANZ where I worked on deals like the Alibaba privatisation, to KKR where I built IC presentations for a $300M fund, to running my own business where I built the entire tech stack from scratch.”

Goal: Within 60 seconds, they should know you’re not a typical developer.

Minutes 5–15

Listen, Don’t Pitch

Turn the conversation to them. Ask: “Before I go deeper into my background, I’d love to understand what you’re looking for. What’s the core problem you’re trying to solve with this CRM project?”

Listen for: Is this about CRM specifically, or broader tech/ops? Are they building or buying? What systems do they currently use? Who are the stakeholders?

Goal: Understand the real scope before you position yourself.

Minutes 15–30

Bridge Your Experience to Their Needs

Based on what they said, select the most relevant stories from your experience bridge. If they mention deal flow → KKR story. If they mention client engagement → STT Mall CRM story. If they mention process improvement → Proudfoot story. If they mention scale challenges → STT Mall 10x efficiency story.

Goal: Every answer connects your past to their future.

Minutes 30–40

Demonstrate Domain Depth

This is where you show you’ve done the work. Reference: entity modelling for family structures, multi-jurisdiction compliance complexity, the build-vs-buy decision framework, integration challenges with custodians. Use vocabulary that signals you belong: “suitability assessment”, “beneficial ownership”, “mandate tracking.”

Goal: They stop thinking of you as an outsider to wealth management.

Minutes 40–50

Plant the AI Seed (Only If Natural)

If the conversation has gone well and they’re engaged, plant one AI idea that connects to a pain point they mentioned. “One thing I’ve been thinking about for your context — with new RMs joining (like Michael and Jennie), the onboarding challenge is getting context on every family fast. There are interesting approaches using relationship intelligence...”

Goal: Leave them thinking about what’s possible, not just what you’ve done.

Last 10 minutes

Close with the First 90 Days

When asked if you have questions: “I do — and I’ve also been thinking about what the first 90 days would look like. My approach is listen-first: two weeks shadowing your team before recommending anything. Would you be open to me sharing that framework?”

Goal: Show you’re already thinking like an employee, not an interviewee.

7 Closing Strategy

The Final Impression

The last 2 minutes determine what they remember. Choose one of these closings based on how the conversation went:

If the conversation went well (energy, engagement, follow-up questions):

“I want to be direct: this is the most exciting opportunity I’ve seen because it sits at the exact intersection of everything I’ve built over 18 years — finance, operations, and technology. I don’t need months to ramp up on your world because I’ve lived in it. I’d love to move forward.”
If they seemed focused on a narrow CRM scope:

“Even if this starts as a CRM project, I see it as the foundation for something much bigger — a technology backbone that helps Raffles scale across markets while keeping the personal touch that UHNW clients expect. I’d love to start with the CRM and prove the value from there.”
If they raised concerns about your background:

“I understand the gaps you might see. I’m not a financial services CRM specialist. What I am is someone who has built a business, led teams, sat in deal rooms, and taught himself to build every system that business needed. That combination of judgment and execution is rare — and it’s exactly what a growing organisation needs when the challenge isn’t just building software, but building the right software.”

After the Interview

Same Day
Send a thank-you email
  • Short (3–4 sentences). Reference one specific moment from the conversation.
  • “I particularly enjoyed our discussion about [X] — it reinforced my conviction that this is a unique opportunity.”
  • If you promised to share anything (a framework, a reference), attach it.
Within 48 Hours
Send a one-pager (optional power move)
  • A concise “Here’s how I’d approach the first 90 days” document
  • Tailored to what they told you in the interview
  • Shows initiative, listening skills, and strategic thinking
  • Only do this if the interview went well — don’t force it if energy was low

8 Pre-Interview Checklist

The Night Before

  • Re-read this strategy page (focus on Core Thesis and Conversation Flow)
  • Review the org chart — know who might interview you
  • Review the latest news — especially Terence Chow, UAE/Rwanda expansion, digital asset fund
  • Prepare 3 questions for THEM (from the Questions to Ask section)
  • Practice the one-sentence thesis out loud until it feels natural
  • Set out clothes, charge devices, confirm meeting details (time, location, format)

Morning Of

  • Re-read just the Core Thesis and Conversation Flow
  • Review the Landmines — what NOT to say
  • Mentally rehearse the first 60 seconds (the credibility opener)
  • Arrive 10 minutes early. Observe the office environment. Note any awards, branding, culture signals.

During the Interview

  • First 60 seconds: establish credibility (Bloomberg, ANZ, KKR, STT Mall COO)
  • Listen more than you talk for the first 15 minutes
  • Every answer bridges your past to their future
  • Use their vocabulary: “families” not “clients”, “mandates” not “requirements”
  • If you don’t know something, say so — then bridge to what you do know
  • Ask at least one question that shows you’ve researched them deeply
  • Close with conviction, not just politeness
